01 Basic Foundational Outline · அடிப்படை கருத்துருவம்

What "Percentage" Actually Means

The word percentage comes from the Latin per centum, meaning "by the hundred" or "out of a hundred." Whenever you meet a percentage, your mind should immediately translate it into a picture: a square, a strip, a jar of one hundred marbles, divided into one hundred equal parts, of which some number are coloured in. A percentage is nothing more, and nothing less, than a fraction whose denominator has been fixed, once and for all, at 100.

A percentage is a frozen fraction. "45%" is simply a shorthand for the fraction 45/100, which is a shorthand for the ratio 45 : 100, which is a shorthand for "45 parts out of every 100 parts of the whole." Every rule in this entire subject is a consequence of that single sentence.

The Three Faces of the Same Number

Grade-6 students should learn, before anything else, that a fraction, a decimal, and a percentage are three costumes worn by the same underlying quantity. 1/2, 0.5, and 50% are not three different ideas — they are one idea photographed from three different angles.

Why the Whole World Uses "Out of 100"

Humans standardised on 100 for the same reason we standardised on metres and kilograms: because comparison is impossible without a common unit. If Arun scores 34 out of 40 in Mathematics and 78 out of 90 in Science, you cannot tell, at a glance, which is the better performance — the two "wholes" are different sizes. But convert both to "out of 100," and instantly they can stand side by side on the same ruler: 85% and 86.7%. Percentage is the great equaliser of fractions with unequal denominators.

First Daily-Life Encounters (for the young learner)

  • School marks: "You scored 90%" — meaning, if the test had been out of 100, you'd have got 90.
  • Battery and signal bars: your phone's 63% battery is literally 63 parts out of 100 of the total charge.
  • Weather: "70% chance of rain" tells you how many times out of a hundred similar weather patterns, rain followed.

The Visual Model Every Teacher Should Draw First

Draw a 10×10 grid — a hundred small squares. Shade a portion. The number of shaded squares is the percentage. This is precisely the animated grid at the top of this document: move the slider, and watch the shaded count and the percentage move together, square for square. This single image is the seed from which every later formula grows — the grid never lies, and whenever a formula feels confusing, redraw the grid.

A percentage on its own, with no "of what," is meaningless. "20% off" only makes sense once you know 20% of the marked price. Students at every level — including postgraduate ones reading financial reports — forget to ask "20% of what base?" This single question prevents almost every classic percentage trap you will meet later in this document.
02 Elementary-Level Formulae · அடிப்படை சூத்திரங்கள்

The Four Core Formulae

Every percentage problem you will ever face — from a Grade 6 worksheet to a postgraduate econometrics paper — is built from combinations of just four elementary moves. Master these four, and the "intermediate," "advanced," and "higher-order" tiers later in this document are simply these same four ideas wearing more elaborate clothing.

Formula 1 — Percentage of a whole Percentage % = (Part ÷ Whole) × 100
Worked Example

Out of 25 students in a class, 20 passed. What percentage passed?
% = (20 ÷ 25) × 100 = 0.8 × 100 = 80%

Formula 2 — Finding the part (percentage of a number) Part = (Percentage ÷ 100) × Whole
Worked Example

Find 15% of 240.
Part = (15 ÷ 100) × 240 = 0.15 × 240 = 36

Formula 3 — Finding the whole (reverse percentage) Whole = (Part ÷ Percentage) × 100
Worked Example

60 is 24% of what number?
Whole = (60 ÷ 24) × 100 = 250

Formula 4 — Percentage change (increase / decrease) % Change = [(New Value − Old Value) ÷ Old Value] × 100
Worked Example

A shirt's price rises from ₹800 to ₹920. Find the percentage increase.
% Change = [(920 − 800) ÷ 800] × 100 = (120 ÷ 800) × 100 = 15% increase

03 Intermediate Problem-Solving with Shortcuts · இடைநிலை சூழல்கள் மற்றும் குறுக்குவழிகள்

Where Percentage Meets Real Transactions

This tier is where percentage stops being an abstract exercise and starts running the world's shops, banks, and newsrooms. Six problem-families dominate this tier: profit & loss, discount, tax, simple interest, mixtures/averages, and successive change. Each gets its formula, a shortcut, and a worked example.

3.1 Profit and Loss

Core relations Profit % = (Profit ÷ Cost Price) × 100  |   Loss % = (Loss ÷ Cost Price) × 100
Selling Price = Cost Price × (1 ± Profit/Loss %/100)
Worked Example

A trader buys a fan for ₹1,200 and sells it for ₹1,380. Find the profit percentage.
Profit = 1380 − 1200 = 180. Profit % = (180 ÷ 1200) × 100 = 15%

Profit and Loss percentages are always calculated on the Cost Price, never on the Selling Price — this single rule resolves most confusion in this chapter.

3.2 Discount

Core relation Selling Price = Marked Price × (1 − Discount%/100)
Worked Example — Successive Discounts

A shop offers "30% off, plus an extra 10% off" on a ₹2,000 jacket. What is the final price? (Note: this is NOT a 40% discount!)
After 30%: 2000 × 0.70 = 1400. After extra 10%: 1400 × 0.90 = ₹1,260 (an effective discount of 37%, not 40%).

Successive percentage changes never simply add up. "30% off then 10% off" is not "40% off." The shortcut formula for this appears below.

⚡ Shortcut: The Successive Percentage-Change Formula

For two successive changes of a% and b% (use negative values for decreases) Net % Change = a + b + (a × b)/100
Applying the shortcut

Discounts of 30% then 10%: here a = −30, b = −10.
Net = −30 + (−10) + (−30×−10)/100 = −40 + 3 = −37% (matches the ₹1,260 answer instantly, no multiplication of large numbers needed).

3.3 Tax (GST/VAT-style problems)

Core relation Price Paid = List Price × (1 + Tax%/100)
Worked Example

An item listed at ₹1,500 attracts 18% GST. Find the final billed amount.
Final = 1500 × 1.18 = ₹1,770

3.4 Simple Interest as a Percentage-Per-Year Problem

Core relation Simple Interest = (Principal × Rate% × Time in years) ÷ 100
Worked Example

₹8,000 is invested at 9% simple interest per year for 3 years. Find the interest earned.
SI = (8000 × 9 × 3) ÷ 100 = ₹2,160

3.5 Mixtures and Weighted Percentage Averages

Worked Example

A class of 30 boys has an average score of 70%, and a class of 20 girls has an average score of 85%. What is the combined average percentage?
Weighted average = (30×70 + 20×85) ÷ 50 = (2100 + 1700) ÷ 50 = 3800 ÷ 50 = 76% — not the naive (70+85)/2 = 77.5%.

Never average two percentages by simply adding and halving unless the two groups are of exactly equal size. Always weight by the size of each group.

04 Problems for Advanced Students · மேம்பட்ட மாணவர்களுக்கான சிக்கல்கள்

Percentage as an Algebraic and Statistical Tool

At the undergraduate tier, percentage stops being arithmetic and becomes algebra: unknowns are embedded inside percentage relationships, and percentages must be combined across time, across categories, and across measurement systems.

4.1 Percentage Points vs. Percentage Change (a crucial distinction)

If an interest rate rises from 8% to 10%, it has risen by 2 percentage points, but by 25% in relative (percentage) terms — because (10−8)/8 × 100 = 25%. Newspapers routinely confuse these two, and advanced students must learn to tell them apart, since financial and policy decisions depend on which one is meant.

4.2 Successive Percentage Change Over Multiple Periods (Compounding of Percentages)

General n-period compounding of a percentage rate r Final Value = Initial Value × (1 + r/100)n
Worked Example — Population Growth

A town's population is 50,000 and grows at 4% per year. Find the population after 3 years.
P = 50000 × (1.04)³ = 50000 × 1.124864 = 56,243 (approx.)

4.3 Reverse-Engineering the Original Value (Back-calculation)

Worked Example

After a 20% pay cut, an employee's salary is ₹36,000. What was the original salary?
36000 = Original × (1 − 0.20) = Original × 0.80 → Original = 36000 ÷ 0.80 = ₹45,000

Students often wrongly reverse a decrease by simply adding back the same percentage (adding 20% of 36,000). That is incorrect, because the 20% cut was taken from a larger original base, not from the reduced value. Always divide by (1 − r/100), never multiply back by (1 + r/100), when reversing a decrease.

4.4 Data Interpretation: Reading Percentages from a Pie Chart

Household Monthly Budget — Percentage Breakdown

Worked Example

If the monthly household income is ₹60,000, and the chart shows 22% spent on Education, find the amount.
Amount = (22/100) × 60000 = ₹13,200

4.5 Percentage Error in Measurement (a bridge to Physics and Engineering)

Definition % Error = (|Measured Value − True Value| ÷ True Value) × 100
Worked Example

The true length of a rod is 200 cm; a student measures 198.5 cm. Find the percentage error.
% Error = (1.5 ÷ 200) × 100 = 0.75%

4.6 Algebraic (Unknown-in-the-Middle) Percentage Equations

Worked Example

A number increased by 25% and then decreased by 20% gives 114. Find the original number x.
x × 1.25 × 0.80 = 114 → x × 1.00 = 114 → x = 114
(Notice: +25% followed by −20% is a coincidence that returns a net multiplier of exactly 1 — a nice example of how successive percentage changes can cancel, but only when the multipliers themselves multiply to 1, not when the raw percentages sum to zero.)

4.7 Combining Percentages Across Different Bases (Ratio & Proportion Bridge)

Worked Example

In an election, Candidate A gets 55% of 40,000 votes cast in Ward 1, and 45% of 60,000 votes cast in Ward 2. Who wins overall, and by what overall percentage?
A's votes = 0.55×40000 + 0.45×60000 = 22000 + 27000 = 49000 out of 100000 total = 49% — A actually loses the overall contest despite winning Ward 1 comfortably, since Ward 2 was larger and there A's share was under half.

This is a classic case of Simpson's Paradox in miniature — good performance in each sub-group does not guarantee a good overall percentage once the sub-groups are unevenly sized. Advanced students meet this idea again in statistics.
05 Higher-Order Thinking (Postgraduate Tier) · உயர்நிலை சிந்தனை

Percentage at the Frontier: Calculus, Finance, and Inference

At the postgraduate level, "percentage" reveals itself as the discrete cousin of a much deeper idea: the relative rate of change. This section connects percentage to calculus, continuous compounding, and statistical inference — the places where research mathematicians and economists actually live.

5.1 From Discrete Percentage Growth to Continuous Growth (the calculus bridge)

When a quantity N grows by a constant percentage rate r every instant rather than once a year, the discrete formula N×(1+r/100)ⁿ is replaced by exponential growth governed by a differential equation:

Continuous percentage growth dN/dt = rN  ⟹   N(t) = N₀ ert

Here, r (as a decimal, e.g. 0.05 for 5%) is precisely a relative or percentage rate of change: r = (1/N)(dN/dt). This is why economists call r the "instantaneous percentage growth rate" — it is the calculus definition of a percentage change happening continuously rather than once a year.

The bridge from school percentage to university calculus is this single identity: percentage rate of change ≈ derivative of the natural logarithm. Formally, the relative rate of change of N is d(ln N)/dt. This is why "percentage growth rates" appear so often expressed as differences in logarithms in econometrics — ln(N_new) − ln(N_old) ≈ percentage change, for small changes.

5.2 Continuous Compounding vs. Annual Compounding — the Limit

The classical limit definition lim (n→∞) (1 + r/n)n = er

Compounding a percentage rate more and more frequently — annually, then monthly, then daily, then every instant — converges to Euler's number e raised to the rate. This single limit is the historical origin of the constant e itself: Jacob Bernoulli discovered it in 1683 while studying compound interest, which is to say, while studying percentages compounding faster and faster.

Simple vs. Compound vs. Continuous Growth of ₹10,000 at 10% over 20 Years

5.3 CAGR — The Postgraduate Finance Standard

Compound Annual Growth Rate CAGR = [(Final Value ÷ Initial Value)1/n − 1] × 100
Worked Example

An investment grows from ₹2,00,000 to ₹4,50,000 over 6 years. Find the CAGR.
CAGR = [(450000/200000)^(1/6) − 1] × 100 = [(2.25)^(0.1667) − 1] × 100 ≈ 14.4% per year

A common analyst's error is to report the total percentage gain (125% here) as if it were an annual rate. CAGR exists precisely to correct that — always distinguish total percentage growth from annualised percentage growth.

5.4 Real vs. Nominal Percentage Returns — the Fisher Relation

Approximate and exact forms Real Rate ≈ Nominal Rate − Inflation Rate  (approximate)
(1 + Real) = (1 + Nominal) ÷ (1 + Inflation)  (exact, Irving Fisher's equation)
Worked Example

A bank offers 8% nominal interest while inflation runs at 5.5%. Find the exact real return.
(1+Real) = 1.08 ÷ 1.055 = 1.0237 → Real ≈ 2.37% (not the naive 2.5% from simple subtraction).

5.5 Percentages and the Base-Rate Fallacy (Bayesian Statistics)

Postgraduate statistics reveals that percentages describing conditional probabilities can be dangerously misleading if the underlying base rate is ignored — this is one of the most important "higher-order" percentage traps in all applied mathematics.

The Classic Base-Rate Problem

A disease affects 1% of a population. A test for it is 95% accurate (correctly identifies both sick and healthy people 95% of the time). If a random person tests positive, what is the actual percentage chance they have the disease?
Out of 10,000 people: 100 are truly sick (1%), of whom 95 test positive. Of the 9,900 healthy people, 5% — that is 495 — also test positive (false positives). So of 95 + 495 = 590 positive results, only 95 are truly sick.
True percentage chance of disease given a positive test = 95 ÷ 590 × 100 ≈ 16.1%, dramatically lower than the "95%" the test's accuracy might suggest.

This is Bayes' Theorem expressed entirely in percentage language, and it is the single most important "percentage trap" a postgraduate reader of medical, legal, or machine-learning statistics can learn to recognise. A high accuracy percentage does not translate directly into a high confidence percentage once the base rate is small.

5.6 Percentage Change of a Ratio — the Delta Method (Research-Level)

When a quantity Q is itself a ratio of two other quantities, Q = A/B, and both A and B change by small percentages a% and b% respectively, the percentage change in Q is approximately:

First-order approximation (used throughout error propagation in the sciences) % Change in Q ≈ a% − b%

This single approximation underlies "percentage error propagation" in physics laboratories, GDP-per-capita calculations in economics (where A = GDP, B = population), and productivity statistics everywhere. It is the discrete shadow of the calculus rule d(ln(A/B)) = d(ln A) − d(ln B).

07 The Shortcut Vault · குறுக்குவழி களஞ்சியம்

Every Shortcut Method in One Place

1. The 10% / 1% Ladder

Find 10% (shift decimal one place), then scale: 5% = half of 10%; 20% = double 10%; 1% = shift decimal two places; 2%, 3%... = multiples of 1%. Almost any "nice" percentage (5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 50, 75) can be built from 10% and 1% in two mental steps.

2. The Flip Trick — x% of y = y% of x

Always choose whichever direction is easier to compute mentally. 4% of 75 is awkward; flipped, it's 75% of 4 = 3. Instant.

3. The Multiplying-Factor Method for Increase/Decrease

Instead of computing the change and adding/subtracting, multiply directly by the factor (1 ± r/100). This avoids a two-step calculation and is essential once problems stack multiple percentage changes together.

4. The Successive-Change Formula

Net % = a + b + ab/100 for two successive percentage changes a% and b% (use negative numbers for decreases). Extend to three changes by applying the formula twice, combining two changes at a time.

5. Fraction-Equivalent Memory Table

Memorising that 1/8 = 12.5%, 1/3 = 33.33%, 1/16 = 6.25%, etc. converts many percentage-of-a-number calculations into simple division, which is often faster than multiplying by a decimal.

6. Reverse-Percentage Division Rule

To undo a percentage decrease of r%, divide by (1 − r/100); to undo an increase of r%, divide by (1 + r/100). Never "add back" or "subtract back" the same percentage — the base has changed.

7. Weighted-Average Shortcut (Alligation)

When mixing two percentages in a known ratio, use the alligation rule: the ratio of quantities mixed is inversely proportional to the distance of each percentage from the resulting mean. This avoids setting up a full weighted-average equation.

8. Log-Based Estimate for Compounding (Rule of 72)

To estimate how many years it takes a quantity to double at a compound percentage rate r% per year, divide 72 by r. At 8% per year, doubling takes roughly 72/8 = 9 years. This shortcut is derived from the natural logarithm of 2 (≈0.693) and is a favourite of postgraduate finance courses for mental estimation.

9. Percentage-Point Awareness Check

Before answering any question involving "rate changed from a% to b%," pause and ask whether the question wants the percentage-point difference (b − a) or the relative percentage change [(b−a)/a × 100]. These numbers are only equal by coincidence.

10. Base-Rate Sanity Check (Bayesian shortcut)

Whenever a percentage describes "accuracy" or "confidence" about a rare event, always build a hypothetical table of 10,000 cases before trusting the percentage at face value — this converts a confusing conditional percentage into simple counting, as shown in Tier 5.5 above.

08 Daily-Life Application Gallery · அன்றாட வாழ்வு பயன்பாடுகள்

Where You Meet Percentage Every Single Day

Shopping

End-of-season sales stack percentages: "flat 40% + extra 20% on billing" — use the successive-change formula, not simple addition.

Banking

Fixed deposits quote an annual percentage rate; your actual maturity value depends on whether interest compounds yearly, quarterly, or monthly.

Nutrition Labels

"%RDA" (Recommended Daily Allowance) on a food packet expresses a nutrient amount as a percentage of what a typical adult needs in a day.

Sports

A batter's strike rate, a bowler's economy conversion, a football team's win percentage, and a basketball player's free-throw percentage are all part-of-whole percentage calculations.

Elections

Vote-share percentages must be combined across constituencies carefully, as in the Simpson's Paradox example in Tier 4.7.

Health

Body fat percentage, oxygen saturation (SpO₂ %), and humidity percentage are all readings against a fixed maximum of 100.

Economics

Inflation, GDP growth, and unemployment are always reported as percentages, and comparing countries requires the "percentage of what base" question from Tier 1.

Exams and Admissions

Cut-off percentages, reservation percentages, and normalisation across different exam boards all rely on the common-base idea introduced in Tier 1.

Foundation (Grade 6–8)

  2. Express 7/20 as a percentage.

  4. 45 is what percentage of 180?

Intermediate (Grade 9–12)

  1. A shopkeeper marks an item 40% above cost price and then gives a 15% discount on the marked price. Find his overall profit percentage.
  2. A number is first increased by 30% and then decreased by 30%. Find the net percentage change.
  3. A mixture of milk and water is 80% milk. If 20 litres of pure water is added to 100 litres of this mixture, find the new percentage of milk.

Advanced (Undergraduate)

  1. A company's revenue grows by 12% in Year 1 and falls by 8% in Year 2. Find the overall percentage change over the two years, and compare it with the naive sum (12% − 8%).
  2. Prices rose by 25%. By what percentage must the new price now fall to return to the original price?
  3. In a survey, candidate X gets 62% of 15,000 votes in District A and 38% of 25,000 votes in District B. Find X's overall percentage across both districts, and state whether X wins overall.
